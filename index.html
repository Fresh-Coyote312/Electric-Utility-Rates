The Problem
Residential electric rate data is scattered across hundreds of regulatory filings, tariff PDFs, and utility informational sheets — making historical analyses difficult.

Rates change constantly — a utility might update its fuel adjustment charge (FAC) every month, adjust energy efficiency riders annually, and file new base rates every few years. Every one of these changes is buried in a different filing, often in different formats across different regulatory commissions. Riders are never standardized between utilities: one utility calls it "Purchased Electricity Adjustment" while another calls it "Fuel Clause." Time-of-Use structures, seasonal splits, and tiered pricing make direct comparisons across utilities nearly impossible.

What This Project Does
This database resolves that problem. Every monthly rate value has been extracted from source filings and standardized into a consistent format: service charge, energy charge, distribution charge, surcharges broken down by rider — all in one structured dataset with up to 14 years of data. The result is a clean, comparable view of residential electric rates.

About Riders

Riders are line-item adjustments added to or subtracted from your base rate. Common examples include:

  • FA (Fuel Adjustment) — reflects changes in fuel generation costs, often updated monthly
  • ECR (Energy Cost Recovery) — recovers above/below budget generation costs
  • DSR (Demand Side Response) — costs for demand response and load management programs
  • EECR / EE — energy efficiency compliance charges
  • Nuclear — nuclear plant decommissioning or operation surcharges
  • TDR (Transmission Demand Response) — transmission-level demand response costs
  • LI (Low Income) — low-income assistance program charges

A negative surcharge means the rider is reducing your rate that month (e.g., a fuel cost credit). The Notes column breaks out every individual rider value so you can see exactly what's driving the total.
